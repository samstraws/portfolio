KPMG Design System

Intro

To expediate and unify KPMG’s internal designs, I researched, designed, and fleshed out a branded design system in Adobe XD.

Problem Space

KPMG is an international tax advisory and consulting company; I worked in the federal consulting branch, which developed projects (such as webpages or apps) for the government. As KPMG built up its design programs and projects, they looked for ways to make their designs cohesive and unified. Designers working on different projects may repeat work that their peers have done and may want to draw from existing designs.

Large tech companies maintain design systems: a centralized library and guidelines that designers draw upon. However, KPMG’s designers were working on various projects, so no one had dedicated time to do this. Atomic Design by Brad Frost outlines a methodology for taking small design parts (e.g. buttons, font, colors) and building out larger elements (e.g. forms, home page), resulting in a cohesive and responsive system. My goal was to create a system that designers could navigate through (like a webpage) to quickly pull or build elements.

Ideation Phase

In my research phase, I had a few questions:

  • How do other companies lay out their design systems?
  • What elements are included in those systems?
  • What elements and themes are relevant to KPMG?
  • How can I make this system useful for my coworkers?

Using a MURAL board and interviewing my team, I researched KPMG’s branding book (colors, typography) and our existing design system – a collection of fonts, colors, and elements. Early on, I established a common language to talk about my concepts. “Elements” are the basics of the system: the visual themes, color, typography. “Components” are small, common features made of elements such as buttons, accordions, or text boxes. “Patterns” are made of multiple components – login pages or navigation bars. Finally, “Data Visuals” are graphics that display data, such as pie charts or bar graphs. I was specifically drawn to Google’s Material’s use of philosophy and shape language. It takes a single shape or idea and builds-- for example, a slant motif to signify a travel brand’s efficiency is used on buttons.

Drawing from my experience in game design, I wanted KPMG’s brand to feel snappy and responsive, instead of weightless – your actions have reactions. I mocked up some buttons that make the user feel like they are pushing a physical object up or down. The existing logo is slanted, so I also drew from that idea of forward motion. Hovering over key objects slant the text. Slightly rounded corners with a mostly rectangular style give it a professional feel.

Content

I also created a structure with reusable components (such as the navigation bar and simple headers) so that designers could easily navigate and build out the file. Inspired by Bootstrap, I created a high level structure of potential elements I wanted to include. I presented my project each week to the team to tweak plans and designs.

Once I settled on the elements and design direction, I developed components and patterns. Inspired by Adobe’s Spectrum and Bootstrap, I developed components to fit specific scenarios. More important components popped more, lesser ones were more subtle. Then, I created content guidelines (based on my principles of simplicity) that show designers how to use the component.

Solution/Reflection

My final solution is in website format, with master components designers can pull from.This project taught me many new skills; I honed prototyping in Adobe XD and took classes on how the backend of Bootstrap functioned. The work I did continues to be used, especially since I made the elements of the design system itself into components.
