What the Flock! Game

Intro

Helped build and design a melee-fighting game from the ground up.

What the Flock was a semester-long project I made as part of an 8-person group in Spring 2021. As a coder and designer, I worked on both the mechanics of the game in Java and the animated sprites in Photoshop. As the team lead, I worked with the designers and coders to organize task flow on Trello. With my group, we also made many writeups selling our game concept and making it appealing to our target audience, the players of the Game Design showcase at the end of the semester.

Problem Space

Our goal was to build a fun, engaging game that incorporated an interesting cooking mechanic and had balanced mechanics. The cooking mechanic centered around an uncooked chicken. The player’s goals would be to 1. survive enemy hordes and 2. cook their chicken by raising its temperature. At any given time, the player’s chef character could either choose to whack enemies with their chicken or put it on the stove, cooking it. Whacking enemies would lower the temperature, while putting it on the stove cooked it.

Animations and Coding

I animated three sprites in Photoshop, for all three environmental traps: the toaster, hot sauce, and cooler. The entire team debated on the designs and concepts for what these separate traps would do. I then followed our pre-determined design guidelines to make trap designs that were appealing and colorful, trying to incorporate basic animation concepts (push and pull, exaggeration). These were my first animations.

For coding, I focused on overall code structure and organization. Because our game was built on older prototype code, much of the organization and logic became obsolete as we added on new features. I often restructured and organized while also adding sprites I had colored in directly.

Other Design Work

As part of the team, I learned to prototype games physically, digitally, and most importantly -- quickly. Game mechanics took lots of time to tweak and adjust. I also dabbled in designing levels that focused on environmental interactions (having two traps in the vicinity of one another, for example). With my team, we broke certain game interactions into “blocks” we could then use to create levels.

Additionally, I created the font on the level home screen and the cutting board background. I hand-lettered the font to match the fun and playful aesthetic established by the two other designers. I wanted an almost comic book-esque look, so I made sure that the curves of the font were still smooth.

Solution

Our final game contains roughly 20 levels, with 3 different environmental traps and 5 different enemy types. To validate the playability of our game, we showcased it at the end of the semester. Test players of all ages played it and gave us feedback.

Reflection

Building a game from the ground up was a large technical and artistic challenge. Many of the problems we encountered were on the logistics side -- organizing which team member was putting out which fire was an ordeal each week, especially on the two week sprints that we went through. Other than that, our biggest challenge was level design. If we had more time, I wish I could better tutorialize our game -- the difficulty grading on the levels is not consistent. I'd also love to get more player feedback and advice about how to teach players how to play the game better.
